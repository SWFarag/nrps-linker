How selective IMLs are toward pairs of modules

  • Here we are trying to explore whether linkers tend to bind multiple pairs of modules (universal linkers) or every unique linker binds a specific pair of modules. Clustering of the ~38k extracted inter-modular linkers (IMLs) using the UCLUST clustering algorithm (Edgar RC.), have shown that the data comprise of precisely 10079 unique linkers (centroids/clusters). 9844 out of 10079 linkers were found to be associated with only a single pair of modules, while the remaining 235 linkers tend to bind multiple distinct pairs of modules (ranging between 2 and up to 13 unique pairs).



  • In order to establish a more rigorous case about our previous analysis, we decided to create a graph with the 993 Inter-modular linkers extracted from the species Mycobacterium Abscessus. The nodes of the graph represent the linkers and edges are constructed between nodes, who only share at least 80% similarity. After building the graph, we applied Louvain community detection algorithm on it (Vincent D. Blondel et.al). The algorithm was able to detect 8 distinct communities. Each community consists of linkers that bind specifically to a single pair of modules. For simplicity purposes, a pair of modules will be represented by their activated substrates (e.g. Ser-Linker-Ala --> Ser-Ala).



Conclusion:

From the aboved mentioned analyses we concluded, that Linkers are highly selective towards pairs of modules and they do not link them randomly.

How conserved are IMLs within and across species:

Here we are trying to investigate, how truly conserved are linkers linking the same pair of modules across and within species. As an example, we took all linkers linking Thr-Val across all species. First we created a graph with the linkers, where nodes refer to linkers and edges are constructed between nodes who only share at least 80% similarity. Then, we applied Louvain community detection algorithm on the graph, while coloring the nodes by the species from which they were extracted. If linkers are globally conserved across species, we would expect to have one large community with multi-colored nodes, referring to the various species. If linkers are conserved only within species, then one would expect multiple communities to be detected, where nodes within each community will have the same color. Based on the figure below, the latter assumption was proven to be the case. It is also worth mentioning, that the same behavior was detected not only for a specific set of pairs but across all unique pairs within our data (398)



Conclusion:

Linkers of the same pair of modules across all species belong to various communities and hence, they are not globally conserved. Moreover, each species tends to dictate its own set of linkers for each pair of modules.
